# 1. Abstract

**Background:** Ambient air pollution is a recognized exacerbating factor for pediatric respiratory conditions, particularly in rapidly urbanizing environments. However, isolating the pollution signal from confounding variables such as demographic susceptibility and temporal healthcare utilization behavior remains challenging in retrospective clinical studies.
**Objective:** To develop an explainable machine learning framework predicting pediatric respiratory hospital admissions and to evaluate the relative contributions of air pollution, temporal metrics, and demographic characteristics.
**Methods:** We conducted a retrospective observational study on pediatric inpatients (aged 2-12 years) admitted to an urban hospital in Ahmedabad, India, between February 2025 and January 2026. Patient records were merged with daily ambient pollution exposure metrics (PM2.5, PM10, NO2). A suite of baseline models was trained, with the best-performing model interpreted using SHapley Additive exPlanations (SHAP) to decode structural drivers of classification.
**Results:** The dataset comprised 1,110 records (21.4% respiratory, 78.6% non-respiratory). An XGBoost classifier demonstrated stable performance (ROC-AUC 0.775, Recall 0.438). SHAP analysis revealed that demographic susceptibility (specifically the 5-7 year age bracket) and temporal patterns (day incident to admission) were the dominating variables predicting respiratory admission. Air pollution, specifically PM2.5 and its lagged exposures, demonstrated a consistent, robust, but secondary predictive contribution with clear directionality proportional to exposure risk.
**Conclusion:** Respiratory admissions emerge from a complex combination of environmental exposure, demographic susceptibility, and temporal healthcare patterns. Ambient air pollution demonstrates a moderate, independent contribution to model predictions, highlighting its persistent role as an exacerbator of pediatric respiratory morbidity alongside overarching behavioral and demographic variables.

# 2. Introduction

Pediatric respiratory conditions place immense strain on urban healthcare infrastructure, particularly in rapidly developing regions like Ahmedabad, India. Given the physiological immaturity of pediatric respiratory immune defenses, young children are uniquely vulnerable to the adverse effects of ambient air pollution, making environmental exposure a critical focal point in public health analytics. 

While associations between particulate matter (e.g., PM2.5, PM10) and respiratory hospitalizations are documented in epidemiological literature, parsing the direct contribution of pollution against confounding behavioral, temporal, and demographic realities is a persistent challenge in retrospective clinical reporting. Healthcare utilization behavior—such as patient-delay phenomena prioritizing weekday over weekend care—alongside inherently varying age-based susceptibilities actively obscure the pure environmental signal.

Traditional linear or multivariate epidemiological techniques often struggle to untangle the non-linear interactions these variables exhibit. Machine learning offers an alternative, yet many predictive models effectively function as "black boxes" lacking clinical interpretability. This dynamic has increasingly driven researchers toward explainable AI (XAI) paradigms, which sacrifice marginal empirical accuracy for explicit structural visibility bridging the algorithmic logic with clinical meaning.

This study implements an explainable machine learning architecture to model pediatric respiratory admissions using real-world retrospective hospital data paired with localized OpenAQ sensor endpoints. Rather than constructing a real-time forecasting tool, this approach utilizes predictive classification mathematically as an engine to tease apart non-linear associations. Utilizing SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations), we map the structural hierarchy of admissions—evaluating if and how ambient air pollution holds physiological predictive weight compared against intense demographic susceptibilities and overarching healthcare temporal behavior.
